Part 2 — Chapter 2: Sinusoids

2-1: Tuning-Fork Experiment

Key idea: Sinusoids aren't just abstract math — they arise naturally from physical systems. A tuning fork vibrates and produces a waveform that matches $\;x(t) = A\cos(\omega_0 t + \varphi)\;$ almost exactly.

An A-440 tuning fork vibrates at 440 Hz, which is "A above middle C." When recorded with a microphone, the waveform oscillates between symmetric limits and repeats with a period of about $T_0 \approx 0.00227$ s.

2-2: Review of Sine and Cosine Functions

Critical Properties (memorize these)

PropertyEquation

Periodicity$\cos(\theta + 2\pi k) = \cos\theta$, for any integer $k$
Evenness of cosine$\cos(-\theta) = \cos\theta$
Oddness of sine$\sin(-\theta) = -\sin\theta$
Zeros of sine$\sin(\pi k) = 0$, for any integer $k$

Key Trigonometric Identities

#Identity

4$\sin(\alpha \pm \beta) = \sin\alpha\cos\beta \pm \cos\alpha\sin\beta$
5$\cos(\alpha \pm \beta) = \cos\alpha\cos\beta \mp \sin\alpha\sin\beta$

Important derived identity:

$$\cos\alpha\;\cos\beta \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\cos(\alpha+\beta) + \tfrac{1}{2}\cos(\alpha-\beta)$$

2-3: Sinusoidal Signals

The Master Formula

$$x(t) = A\cos(\omega_0\, t + \varphi) = A\cos(2\pi f_0\, t + \varphi)$$

The Three Parameters

SymbolNameUnitsRole
$A$Amplitude(same as signal)Signal oscillates between $+A$ and $-A$
$\omega_0$Radian frequencyrad/sRate of oscillation
$f_0 = \omega_0/2\pi$Cyclic frequencyHzCycles per second
$\varphi$PhaseradShifts the signal in time

2-3.1 — Frequency and Period

$$T_0 = \frac{2\pi}{\omega_0} = \frac{1}{f_0}$$

$T_0$ = period (seconds per cycle), $f_0$ = frequency (cycles per second). They are reciprocals.

Special case: $f_0 = 0$ gives $x(t) = A\cos(0) = A$ — a constant, called DC.

2-3.2 — Phase and Time Shift

$$\varphi = -\omega_0\, t_1 = -2\pi\!\left(\frac{t_1}{T_0}\right)$$
$$t_1 = \frac{-\varphi}{\omega_0} = \frac{-\varphi}{2\pi f_0} = \frac{-\varphi\, T_0}{2\pi}$$
Watch the signs: A positive time shift $t_1 > 0$ (delay) gives a negative phase, and vice versa. The principal value of phase always lies in $(-\pi,\;\pi]$.
Example
$x(t)=20\cos\!\bigl(2\pi(40)t - 0.4\pi\bigr)$
$A=20,\; f_0=40\text{ Hz},\; \varphi=-0.4\pi$
$T_0 = 1/40 = 0.025\text{ s}$
$t_1 = -(-0.4\pi)\big/(2\pi\cdot 40) = 0.005\text{ s}$ (delayed by 5 ms)

2-4: Sampling and Plotting Sinusoids

Key question: How many samples per period do we need?

Samples/PeriodQuality

Remarkable result: A cosine can be perfectly reconstructed with slightly more than 2 samples per period (the Nyquist rate). This is explored in Chapter 4.

2-5: Complex Exponentials and Phasors  (Critical Section)

2-5.1 — Complex Numbers Review

Cartesian (Rectangular) Form

$$z = x + jy, \qquad x = \operatorname{Re}\{z\},\quad y = \operatorname{Im}\{z\}, \quad j = \sqrt{-1}$$

Polar Form

$$z = r\,e^{j\theta}, \qquad r = |z| = \sqrt{x^2+y^2},\quad \theta = \arg(z) = \arctan\!\left(\tfrac{y}{x}\right)$$

Converting Between Forms

Polar → CartesianCartesian → Polar
$x = r\cos\theta$
$y = r\sin\theta$		 $r = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}$
$\theta = \text{atan2}(y,\,x)$

Euler's Formula  (The Most Important Equation)

$$\boxed{e^{j\theta} = \cos\theta + j\sin\theta}$$

2-5.2 — Complex Exponential Signals

$$z(t) = A\,e^{j(\omega_0 t + \varphi)} = A\cos(\omega_0 t+\varphi) + jA\sin(\omega_0 t+\varphi)$$
The key relationship: $$x(t) = \operatorname{Re}\!\left\{A\,e^{j(\omega_0 t + \varphi)}\right\} = A\cos(\omega_0 t + \varphi)$$ A real cosine signal is the real part of a complex exponential.

2-5.3 — The Phasor (Complex Amplitude)

Define the phasor:

$$X = A\,e^{j\varphi}$$

Then:

$$z(t) = X\,e^{j\omega_0 t}, \qquad x(t) = \operatorname{Re}\!\left\{X\,e^{j\omega_0 t}\right\}$$

What the phasor tells you:

  • $|X| = A$ — the amplitude
  • $\angle X = \varphi$ — the phase
  • The frequency $\omega_0$ is separate from the phasor

Rotating phasor interpretation: $X\,e^{j\omega_0 t}$ traces a circle of radius $A$ in the complex plane. The real cosine is the projection onto the real axis.

  • $\omega_0 > 0$: counterclockwise rotation
  • $\omega_0 < 0$: clockwise rotation

2-5.4 — Inverse Euler Formulas  (Critical)

$$\cos\theta = \frac{e^{j\theta}+e^{-j\theta}}{2}$$
$$\sin\theta = \frac{e^{j\theta}-e^{-j\theta}}{2j}$$

Applied to a cosine signal:

$$A\cos(\omega_0 t+\varphi) = \tfrac{1}{2}X\,e^{j\omega_0 t} + \tfrac{1}{2}X^*e^{-j\omega_0 t}$$
Huge insight: A real cosine is the sum of two complex exponentials:
  • One at positive frequency $+\omega_0$ with amplitude $\tfrac{1}{2}X$
  • One at negative frequency $-\omega_0$ with amplitude $\tfrac{1}{2}X^*$
This is why negative frequencies exist in the spectrum.

2-6: Phasor Addition  (Very Important for Exams)

The Problem

Add $N$ cosine signals that all share the same frequency:

$$\sum_{k=1}^{N} A_k\cos(\omega_0 t + \varphi_k) = A\cos(\omega_0 t + \varphi)$$

The result is always a single cosine at the same frequency.

The Phasor Addition Rule

$$\sum_{k=1}^{N} A_k\,e^{j\varphi_k} = A\,e^{j\varphi}$$

Steps:

  1. Get the phasor for each signal: $X_k = A_k\,e^{j\varphi_k}$
  2. Convert each to rectangular: $X_k = x_k + jy_k$
  3. Add real parts and imaginary parts
  4. Convert the result back to polar to get $A$ and $\varphi$
Worked Example

Given:

$$x_1(t)=1.7\cos(20\pi t + 70^\circ), \qquad x_2(t)=1.9\cos(20\pi t + 200^\circ)$$

Both are 10 Hz signals.

Step 1 — Phasors:

$$X_1 = 1.7\,e^{j\,70\pi/180}, \qquad X_2 = 1.9\,e^{j\,200\pi/180}$$

Step 2 — Convert to rectangular:

$$X_1 = 0.5814 + j\,1.5975, \qquad X_2 = -1.7854 - j\,0.6498$$

Step 3 — Add:

$$X_3 = -1.204 + j\,0.9477$$

Step 4 — Convert to polar:

$$X_3 = 1.5322\,e^{j\,141.79\pi/180}$$

Result:

$$x_3(t) = 1.5322\cos(20\pi t + 141.79^\circ)$$

2-7: Physics of the Tuning Fork

Hooke's law + Newton's second law gives a second-order ODE:

$$m\,\frac{d^2 x(t)}{dt^2} = -k\,x(t)$$

Guess $x(t)=A\cos(\omega_0 t)$; the second derivative is $-\omega_0^2 A\cos(\omega_0 t)$. Substituting:

$$\omega_0 = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}$$

Physical meaning:

  • Stiffer fork (larger $k$) ⇒ higher frequency
  • Heavier fork (larger $m$) ⇒ lower frequency

The general solution $x(t)=A\cos(\omega_0 t + \varphi)$ satisfies the ODE for any $A$ and $\varphi$. Only $\omega_0$ is set by the physics. The same math applies to mass-spring systems, pendulums, and LC circuits.

Chapter 2 — Key Takeaways

  1. A sinusoid is defined by 3 parameters: amplitude $A$, frequency $\omega_0$, phase $\varphi$.
  2. Euler's formula connects complex exponentials to trig: $e^{j\theta}=\cos\theta+j\sin\theta$.
  3. A phasor $X=Ae^{j\varphi}$ captures amplitude and phase.
  4. A real cosine = sum of two complex exponentials at $\pm$ frequencies.
  5. Phasor addition lets you add same-frequency sinusoids easily.
  6. Sinusoids arise from second-order differential equations in physics.

Part 3 — Chapter 3: Spectrum Representation

3-1: The Spectrum of a Sum of Sinusoids

The Big Idea: Any signal made from sinusoids is completely described by listing the frequency, amplitude, and phase of each component. This collection of information is the spectrum.

General Sum of Sinusoids

$$x(t) = A_0 + \sum_{k=1}^{N} A_k\cos(2\pi f_k t + \varphi_k)$$

Using inverse Euler, each cosine splits into two complex exponentials:

$$x(t) = X_0 + \sum_{k=1}^{N}\left[\frac{X_k}{2}\,e^{j2\pi f_k t} + \frac{X_k^*}{2}\,e^{-j2\pi f_k t}\right]$$

The $a_k$ Notation

$$a_k = \begin{cases} A_0 & k=0 \\[4pt] \tfrac{1}{2}A_k\,e^{j\varphi_k} & k\neq 0 \end{cases}$$

This gives the compact synthesis formula:

$$x(t) = \sum_{k=-N}^{N} a_k\,e^{j2\pi f_k t}$$

Spectrum Plot

  • Draw vertical lines at each frequency $f_k$
  • Height = $|a_k|$ (magnitude)
  • Label each line with the complex value of $a_k$
Conjugate symmetry: For a real signal, $a_{-k} = a_k^*$. Negative-frequency components are always conjugates of their positive-frequency partners.
Example: Two-Sided Spectrum

For $x(t) = 10 + 14\cos(200\pi t - \tfrac{\pi}{3}) + 8\cos(500\pi t + \tfrac{\pi}{2})$:

Frequency (Hz)$a_k$
$0$$10$
$100$$7\,e^{-j\pi/3}$
$-100$$7\,e^{j\pi/3}$
$250$$4\,e^{j\pi/2}$
$-250$$4\,e^{-j\pi/2}$

3-2: Sinusoidal Amplitude Modulation

3-2.1 — Multiplying Sinusoids

Multiplying two sinusoids produces a sum at new frequencies (use inverse Euler).

Example
$$x(t) = \cos(\pi t)\;\sin(10\pi t)$$ After expansion: $$x(t) = \tfrac{1}{2}\cos\!\bigl(11\pi t - \tfrac{\pi}{2}\bigr) + \tfrac{1}{2}\cos\!\bigl(9\pi t - \tfrac{\pi}{2}\bigr)$$

Neither original frequency (0.5 Hz, 5 Hz) appears — only 5.5 Hz and 4.5 Hz.

3-2.2 — Beat Notes

Sum of two equal-amplitude sinusoids with close frequencies:

$$\cos(2\pi f_1 t) + \cos(2\pi f_2 t) = 2\cos(2\pi\Delta\!f\;t)\;\cos(2\pi f_c\,t)$$

where $f_c = \tfrac{1}{2}(f_1+f_2)$ and $\Delta\!f = \tfrac{1}{2}(f_2-f_1)$.

What you hear: A tone at $f_c$ that fades in and out at rate $2\Delta\!f$. The closer the frequencies, the slower the beating. Musicians use this to tune instruments.

3-2.3 — Amplitude Modulation (AM)

$$x(t) = v(t)\;\cos(2\pi f_c\,t)$$

$v(t)$ is the voice/music signal; $f_c$ is the high-frequency carrier. A DC offset in $v(t)$ keeps the envelope positive for easy detection.

3-2.4 — AM Spectrum

The AM spectrum consists of frequency-shifted copies of the spectrum of $v(t)$:
  • One copy centered at $+f_c$
  • One copy centered at $-f_c$
  • Each scaled by $\tfrac{1}{2}$

3-2.5 — Bandwidth

Bandwidth = range of frequencies occupied by the signal.

For AM: $\text{BW} = 2\Delta\!f$. AM radio stations are assigned a carrier and a 10 kHz bandwidth — this is frequency-division multiplexing.

3-3: Operations on the Spectrum

3-3.1 — Scaling or Adding a Constant

$$\gamma\,x(t) \;\longleftrightarrow\; \gamma\,a_k \quad\text{(all frequencies unchanged)}$$
$$x(t) + c \;\longleftrightarrow\; \text{DC component becomes } a_0 + c$$

3-3.2 — Adding Signals

  • If both signals have a component at the same frequency: add their complex amplitudes (phasor addition).
  • If a frequency appears in only one signal: copy it directly.
  • Complex amplitudes at the same frequency might cancel.

3-3.3 — Time Shift

$$y(t) = x(t - \tau_d) \;\longleftrightarrow\; b_k = a_k\,e^{-j2\pi f_k\,\tau_d}$$

Delaying a signal changes only the phases — magnitudes and frequencies are unchanged.

Example
Delay $6\cos(250\pi t)$ by $\tau_d = 2$ ms (quarter period):
Phase change at $f=125$ Hz: $e^{-j250\pi(0.002)} = e^{-j\pi/2} = -j$.
Result: $6\sin(250\pi t)$ — a quarter-cycle delay turns cosine into sine.

3-3.4 — Differentiation

$$y(t) = \frac{d}{dt}x(t) \;\longleftrightarrow\; b_k = (j2\pi f_k)\,a_k$$
  • Kills DC (multiplied by 0)
  • Boosts high frequencies
  • Adds $\pi/2$ phase shift (factor of $j$)

3-3.5 — Frequency Shifting

Multiplying by a complex exponential:

$$y(t)=Ae^{j\varphi}\,e^{j2\pi f_c t}\;x(t) \;\longleftrightarrow\; \{(f_k+f_c,\; a_k\,Ae^{j\varphi})\}$$

Multiplying by a real cosine creates two shifted copies:

$$y(t)=x(t)\;A\cos(2\pi f_c t + \varphi) \;\longleftrightarrow\; \text{spectrum shifted }\pm f_c,\;\text{each scaled by }\tfrac{A}{2}e^{\pm j\varphi}$$

3-4: Periodic Waveforms

Harmonic Frequencies

$$x(t) = A_0 + \sum_{k=1}^{N} A_k\cos(2\pi k F_0\,t + \varphi_k)$$

where $f_k = kF_0$ is the $k$th harmonic and $F_0 = 1/T_0$ is the fundamental frequency.

Finding the Fundamental Frequency

$$F_0 = \gcd(f_1,\; f_2,\; \ldots,\; f_N)$$
Example
Frequencies: 12, 20, 60 Hz ⇒ $F_0 = \gcd(12,20,60)=4$ Hz.
12 Hz is the 3rd harmonic, 20 Hz the 5th, 60 Hz the 15th.
Periodicity requires harmonic frequencies. If the frequencies have no common divisor (e.g., irrational ratios), the signal is not periodic.

Complex Exponential Form

$$x(t) = \sum_{k=-N}^{N} a_k\,e^{j2\pi k F_0 t}$$

3-5: Fourier Series  (Very Important)

Core question: Can every periodic signal be written as a sum of harmonically related sinusoids?
Answer: Yes (with possibly infinite terms).

Fourier Synthesis (spectrum → signal)

$$x(t) = \sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty} a_k\,e^{j(2\pi/T_0)\,k\,t}$$

Fourier Analysis (signal → spectrum)

$$a_k = \frac{1}{T_0}\int_0^{T_0} x(t)\;e^{-j(2\pi/T_0)\,k\,t}\;dt$$

DC Component ($k=0$)

$$a_0 = \frac{1}{T_0}\int_0^{T_0} x(t)\;dt = \text{average value of }x(t)\text{ over one period}$$

Example: Full-Wave Rectified Sine (FWRS)

Signal: $x(t) = |\sin(2\pi t/T_1)|$, period $T_0 = T_1/2$.

After evaluating the Fourier integral:

$$a_k = \frac{2}{\pi(1 - 4k^2)}$$
$k$$a_k$Approximate value
0$2/\pi$$0.6366$ (DC)
1$-2/(3\pi)$$-0.2122$
2$-2/(15\pi)$$-0.04244$
3$-2/(35\pi)$$-0.01819$

All coefficients are real. Magnitudes fall off as $k^{-2}$.

  • $N=4$ harmonics: rough approximation
  • $N=9$ harmonics: very close to the true signal

3-6: Time–Frequency Spectrum

Real signals (music, speech) have frequency content that changes with time. A single spectrum doesn't capture this.

Piano Key Frequencies

Adjacent keys: frequency ratio $r = 2^{1/12} \approx 1.0595$. Reference: A-440 = key 49.

$$f(\text{key }n) = 440 \times 2^{(n-49)/12} \;\text{Hz}$$

Middle C (key 40): $f = 440 \times 2^{-9/12} \approx 261.6$ Hz.

The Spectrogram

A spectrogram is a 2-D plot: x-axis = time, y-axis = frequency, color/darkness = amplitude. Computed by doing Fourier analysis on short overlapping segments of the signal.

3-7: Frequency Modulation & Chirp Signals

Generalized Cosine

$$x(t) = A\cos\!\bigl(\psi(t)\bigr)$$

where $\psi(t)$ is the angle function.

Instantaneous Frequency

$$f_i(t) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\,\frac{d\psi(t)}{dt} \quad\text{(Hz)}$$
Angle function $\psi(t)$$f_i(t)$Signal type
$\omega_0 t + \varphi$$f_0$ (constant)Sinusoid
$2\pi\mu t^2 + 2\pi f_0 t + \varphi$$2\mu t + f_0$ (linear)Chirp

Chirp (Linear FM) Signal

Sweeps from $f_1$ to $f_2$ during $0 \le t \le T_2$:

$$f_i(t) = \frac{f_2 - f_1}{T_2}\;t + f_1$$
$$\psi(t) = \pi\!\left(\frac{f_2-f_1}{T_2}\right)t^2 + 2\pi f_1\,t + \varphi$$
Example: Chirp from 300 Hz to 800 Hz in 0.5 s
$$f_i(t) = 1000t + 300\;\text{Hz}$$ $$\psi(t) = 1000\pi t^2 + 600\pi t + \varphi$$ $$x(t) = \cos(1000\pi t^2 + 600\pi t + \varphi)$$ The spectrogram shows a diagonal line from $(0,\;300)$ to $(0.5,\;800)$.

Chapter 3 — Key Takeaways

  1. The spectrum = set of $(f_k,\,a_k)$ pairs representing a signal.
  2. Two-sided spectrum includes $\pm$ frequencies; real signals have conjugate symmetry.
  3. Multiplying sinusoids produces new frequencies (sum and difference).
  4. AM shifts spectrum copies to $\pm f_c$.
  5. Bandwidth = range of frequencies in the signal.
  6. Time shift changes phases only.
  7. Differentiation boosts high frequencies, kills DC.
  8. Frequency shifting moves the entire spectrum.
  9. Periodic signals have harmonic spectra (integer multiples of $F_0$).
  10. Fourier series decomposes any periodic signal into harmonics.
  11. Spectrograms show time-varying frequency content.
  12. Chirp signals have linearly varying instantaneous frequency.

Sinusoid Basics

$$x(t) = A\cos(2\pi f_0 t + \varphi), \qquad T_0 = \frac{1}{f_0}, \qquad \varphi = -2\pi\!\left(\frac{t_1}{T_0}\right)$$

Euler & Inverse Euler

$$e^{j\theta} = \cos\theta + j\sin\theta$$ $$\cos\theta = \frac{e^{j\theta}+e^{-j\theta}}{2}, \qquad \sin\theta = \frac{e^{j\theta}-e^{-j\theta}}{2j}$$

Phasor Representation

$$X = Ae^{j\varphi}, \qquad x(t) = \operatorname{Re}\!\left\{Xe^{j\omega_0 t}\right\}$$

Cosine as Two Exponentials

$$A\cos(\omega_0 t + \varphi) = \tfrac{1}{2}Xe^{j\omega_0 t} + \tfrac{1}{2}X^*e^{-j\omega_0 t}$$

Phasor Addition

$$\sum_k A_k\cos(\omega_0 t+\varphi_k) = A\cos(\omega_0 t + \varphi), \quad\text{where}\quad Ae^{j\varphi} = \sum_k A_k e^{j\varphi_k}$$

$$\textbf{Synthesis:}\quad x(t) = \sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty} a_k\,e^{j2\pi kF_0 t}$$ $$\textbf{Analysis:}\quad a_k = \frac{1}{T_0}\int_0^{T_0}x(t)\,e^{-j2\pi kF_0 t}\,dt$$ $$\textbf{DC:}\quad a_0 = \frac{1}{T_0}\int_0^{T_0}x(t)\,dt$$

$$F_0 = \gcd(f_1,\;f_2,\;\ldots,\;f_N), \qquad T_0 = \frac{1}{F_0}$$

Spectrum Operations

$$\text{Scaling:}\quad \gamma\,x(t) \;\longleftrightarrow\; \gamma\,a_k$$ $$\text{Time shift:}\quad x(t-\tau) \;\longleftrightarrow\; a_k\,e^{-j2\pi f_k \tau}$$ $$\text{Derivative:}\quad \frac{d}{dt}x(t) \;\longleftrightarrow\; (j2\pi f_k)\,a_k$$ $$\text{Freq.\ shift:}\quad x(t)\,e^{j2\pi f_c t} \;\longleftrightarrow\; a_k \;\text{at}\; (f_k + f_c)$$

Instantaneous Frequency & Chirp

$$f_i(t) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\frac{d\psi}{dt}, \qquad \mu = \frac{f_2 - f_1}{T_2}$$ $$x(t) = \cos\!\left(\pi\mu\,t^2 + 2\pi f_1\,t + \varphi\right)$$

$$\text{Addition:}\quad (x_1+jy_1)+(x_2+jy_2) = (x_1+x_2)+j(y_1+y_2)$$ $$\text{Multiplication:}\quad r_1 e^{j\theta_1}\cdot r_2 e^{j\theta_2} = r_1 r_2\,e^{j(\theta_1+\theta_2)}$$ $$\text{Conjugate:}\quad (re^{j\theta})^* = re^{-j\theta}$$ $$\text{Magnitude:}\quad |x+jy| = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}$$
